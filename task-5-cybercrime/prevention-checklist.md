# Part B - Prevention Checklist

## 'Stay Safe Online' Checklist for Indian College Students

Living in a digital-first environment means your smartphone is essentially your identity and your
wallet. Follow these eight actionable steps to stay protected:

1. The "Golden Rule" of UPI: Never enter your UPI PIN or scan a QR code to receive
money. If a "buyer" or "friend" asks you to do this, it is a scam. 

2. Limit Your Digital Footprint: Avoid sharing your phone number, Aadhaar details, or
college ID on public social media comments or untrusted websites.

3. Verify Before You Click: Before clicking a link for a "free internship," "scholarship," or
"discount," long-press the link to preview the URL. Check for misspellings like
https://www.google.com/search?q=g00gle.com or amaxon.in.

4. Use Official Apps Only: Download banking, UPI, and social media apps only from the
Google Play Store or Apple App Store. Avoid "Modded" or "Pro" APKs from third-party
websites. 

5. Set Daily Transaction Limits: Use your bank's mobile app to set a daily limit on UPI
and ATM transactions. This ensures that even if a breach occurs, the damage is capped.

6. Public Wi-Fi is Public: Never perform financial transactions or log into sensitive
accounts (like Gmail or LinkedIn) using free canteen or railway station Wi-Fi without a
trusted VPN.

7. Enable Biometric App Locks: Go beyond your phone's screen lock. Enable separate
fingerprint or face-recognition locks for your WhatsApp, Gmail, and all payment apps.

8. Beware of "Digital Arrest" Scams: If you receive a call from someone claiming to be
from the CBI, Police, or FedEx saying your Aadhaar is linked to a crime, hang up
immediately. Real law enforcement will never "arrest" you over a video call.

## Official Reporting Channels

If you suspect fraud or have lost money, every minute counts:
1. Helpline: Call 1930 immediately to report financial cyber fraud.
2. Online Portal: File a formal complaint at cybercrime.gov.in.

## Personal Reflection

What surprised me most during this research was the sheer psychological sophistication of
"Digital Arrest" and "QR Code" scams. It isn't just a technical hack; it's a high-pressure
performance designed to bypass the common sense of even highly educated individuals. I was
particularly startled by how scammers impersonate high-ranking officials to create a sense of
panic that overrides a student's logical thinking.

### Habit Change: 
As a result, I am immediately changing how I handle app permissions. I
realized I've been granting "Contacts" and "SMS" access to almost every app I download. I will
now conduct a "Permission Audit" on my phone and revoke access for any app that doesn't
strictly need it to function, significantly reducing the risk of data scraping and OTP interception.
